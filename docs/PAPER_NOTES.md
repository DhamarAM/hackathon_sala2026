## 1. Galápagos species and frequencies (VERIFY in our frontend)

The papers confirm that Galápagos waters host **at least 23 cetacean species**, of which **14 are residents or common visitors**. They include:
- 9 delphinids (dolphin family)
- 1 beaked whale — `Be` in our config
- 1 sperm whale (*Physeter macrocephalus* — **NOT in our SPECIES_MAP**)
- 3 balaenopterids (*B. musculus* blue whale, *B. edeni* Bryde's whale — **Bryde's NOT in SPECIES_MAP**)

**Correct frequency ranges by taxon** (from Paper 1, table):

| Group | Frequency | Typical sounds |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Baleen whales (Mysticetes) | **10 Hz – 4 kHz** | Tonal moans, upcalls, structured songs |
| Toothed whales (Odontocetes) | **2 kHz – 160+ kHz** | Echolocation clicks, burst pulses, FM whistles |
| Fish (Sciaenidae, etc.) | **100 Hz – 3 kHz** | LF choruses, knocks, grunts |
| Invertebrates (shrimp, etc.) | **2 kHz – 200+ kHz** | Broadband transient snaps |

**Frontend impact:** Our bands in `config.js` are:
- `infrasonic_whales: 10-100 Hz` — This is **too narrow**. Mysticetes reach up to 4 kHz.
- `low_freq_fish: 50-500 Hz` — Reasonable, but overlaps with boats (100-1000 Hz).
- `mid_freq_dolphins: 500-5000 Hz` — Whistles are actually at 5-20 kHz.
- `high_freq_clicks: 5-24 kHz` — OK for pilot (48 kHz SR), but real clicks are >100 kHz.

**Action:** Do not change the bands (they come from the backend `analyze_marine_audio.py`), but **in the UI we should explain** that these are simplified analysis bands for the 48 kHz SR of the Pilot, not the full biological ranges. This demonstrates scientific sophistication in the pitch.

## 2. The Humpback model — why 99/100 detects

Papers 2 and 3 explain that the Google Humpback model works with audio **resampled to 10 kHz** and is a binary detector (yes/no per 1-second window). The papers mention:

> "Even a negative result ('no humpbacks detected') is useful information."

Humpback whales produce songs between **100 Hz and 4 kHz**, with harmonics up to 8 kHz. The model was trained on North Pacific humpback songs. Given that San Cristóbal Bay has a lot of **boat noise at 100-1000 Hz** that partially *overlaps* with humpback frequencies, it is plausible that the model generates false positives from boat noise.

**Frontend impact:** Our landing page says "99 Humpback Signals" as if it were an achievement. This is probably a model artifact. We should:
1. Show `fraction_above_threshold` (we currently show it in AnalysisPanel — good)
2. Add context: "Signals consistent with humpback pattern (not verified by experts)"
3. Emphasize that `humpback_detected=true` **does not confirm presence** — only that the score exceeds a configurable threshold

## 3. The 12 classes of the Multispecies Model — vocalizations are types, not species

Paper 1 confirms that the Google Multispecies model classifies **12 whale/dolphin classes**. Our data contains:

**Species (7):** Oo (Orca), Mn (Humpback), Eg (Right), Bp (Fin), Bm (Blue), Ba (Minke), Be (Beaked)
**Vocalizations (5):** Call, Echolocation, Gunshot, Upcall, Whistle

**Important context we should display:**
- `Echolocation` = echolocation clicks from odontocetes (dolphins, orcas, sperm whales)
- `Call` = generic whale call (not species-specific)
- `Upcall` = ascending call typical of right whale (*Eg*)
- `Gunshot` = impulsive gunshot-like sound (right whale at surface)
- `Whistle` = tonal dolphin/whale whistle

**Action:** Already identified in FRONTEND_AUDIT.md. Update SPECIES_MAP and show vocalizations as a separate category from species.

## 4. Spectrograms — what they actually represent

The papers detail that a standard spectrogram is generated with:
- **STFT** (Short-Time Fourier Transform) with ~20ms Hamming windows and ~10ms hop
- **Logarithmic scale** (dB) on the magnitude axis
- **Mel filterbank** (128 or 256 filters) optionally on the frequency axis

Our spectrograms (PNG files generated by `analyze_marine_audio.py`) are static images. Papers 1 and 3 emphasize that **PCEN** (Per-Channel Energy Normalization) is better than log for suppressing stationary noise. This does not directly affect the frontend, but it is a point to mention in the pitch:

> "Our spectrograms use standard logarithmic normalization. For future iterations, PCEN can be implemented which reduces false positives by a factor of 5-50x (according to the literature)."

## 5. The correct temporal hierarchy of SoundTrap files

Paper 3 details the naming convention:
- **Pilot:** `YYMMDD_sequence.wav` → `190806_3754` = August 6, 2019, sequence 3754
- **Unit 6478:** `6478.YYMMDDHHMMSS.wav` → `6478.230723151251` = Jul 23, 2023, 15:12:51
- **Unit 5783:** same pattern as 6478

**Critical data point:** Timestamps in Pilot filenames **are in local time**, not UTC. The SoundTrap's internal clock is in UTC, but upon offload it is converted to the PC's time zone.

**Frontend impact:** If we implement temporal patterns (time of day, date), we must:
1. Parse `YYMMDD` from the filename as a date (we already have this as an idea in FRONTEND_AUDIT)
2. Warn that the time may not be exact UTC
3. For the Pilot, we only have the date, not the time (the `sequence` does not give the time)

## 7. Similarity Search / Agile Modeling — differentiating idea

Paper 1 describes a complete **"similarity search frontend"** architecture where:
1. Embeddings are extracted from each audio window with a foundation model (Perch 2.0)
2. They are stored in a vector database (Milvus)
3. The user selects a "seed" sound and searches for similar ones by cosine distance
4. Active learning: the biologist validates results, a logistic classifier is trained instantly

**This could be our differentiating feature for the pitch.** Although we cannot implement the backend vector DB in this hackathon, we can:
- Show in the frontend a "Find Similar Recordings" UI that groups files by multispecies score
- Simulate an embedding space with the scores we already have (7 scoring dimensions = vector)
- Mention in the pitch that this is the basis for an "Agile Modeling" workflow

## 8. Acoustic indices we should consider

Papers 2 and the hackathon README mention these standard indices:
- **Shannon entropy** — soundscape diversity (high = many types of sound)
- **NDSI** (Normalized Difference Soundscape Index) — bio vs anthropogenic ratio.
  **IMPORTANT:** Default NDSI ranges in `scikit-maad` are for forests (bio: 2-8 kHz, human: 1-2 kHz). **For ocean, they should be:** bio: 2-20 kHz, human: 100-1000 Hz
- **ACI** (Acoustic Complexity Index) — moment-to-moment spectral variability

Our backend already calculates `band_analysis` with 4 bands and energy metrics. These are simplified analogues, but not the standard indices from the literature. For the pitch, we can:
1. Mention that our analysis bands cover the key frequencies
2. Propose NDSI and ACI as future improvements
3. Show that we understand the defaults do not apply to ocean

## 9. Uncalibrated data — disclaimer needed

Paper 3 emphasizes:

> "SoundTrap provides end-to-end calibration. Convert cal_dB to ratio and multiply the signal to obtain µPa."

Our data **are NOT calibrated**. The energies in `band_analysis` are relative, not in absolute µPa. The hackathon README also says: "all sound levels are relative."

**Frontend action:** In the Band Energy Chart or wherever we display dB, add label: "Relative dB (not calibrated to µPa)". This shows scientific rigor.

## 11. Bioacoustic frontend architecture (from Paper 1)

Paper 1 describes the current reference platforms:

| Platform | Stack | What it does |
|-----------|-------|------------|
| Audio Atlas | WebGL + React + DeepScatter | Contrastive embedding exploration |
| A2O Search Platform | Next.js + React + Milvus | Similarity search over petabytes |
| Geono-Cluster | JavaScript + D3.js | Visual cluster refinement (drag-and-drop) |
| PAMGuard | Java + SQLite | Industry standard for .sud, classic detection |
| OpenSoundscape | Python + PyTorch | CNN training, spectrograms, RIBBIT |

Our stack (React + Vite + Chart.js) is aligned with what the literature recommends for bioacoustic frontends. To scale, we would need:
- **WebGL** (DeepScatter or similar) to visualize thousands of embedding points
- **Vector database** for similarity search
- Integration with **wavesurfer.js** for interactive playback
